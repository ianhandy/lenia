LENIA

Lenia is a continuous cellular automaton — a mathematical framework where smooth kernels and growth functions replace the binary rules of Conway's Game of Life. The result: lifelike organisms that move, pulsate, rotate, and self-replicate across a continuous field.

Discovered by Bert Wang-Chak Chan in 2018, Lenia has been published in multiple academic journals and reveals that complex, lifelike behavior emerges from remarkably simple continuous math.

How It Works

Each cell holds a continuous value (0–1) representing "aliveness." A ring-shaped kernel sums the neighborhood, and a Gaussian growth function determines whether the cell grows or shrinks. The kernel radius (R), growth center (μ), and growth width (σ) together define the creature's "DNA."

Interaction

Clicking injects a nutrient pulse -- a temporary region of favorable growth conditions that nearby organisms are drawn toward. The stimulus decays over time. This lets you guide and sustain existing life rather than conjuring new organisms from nothing.

Creatures

Each preset loads a different organism with tuned parameters. Orbium is the classic glider. Geminium self-replicates. Primordia seeds random soup and lets you watch what emerges -- the most surprising mode.

Parameters

R (kernel radius) controls how far each cell "sees." mu (growth center) sets the optimal neighborhood density -- too sparse or too dense and cells die. sigma (growth width) controls how tolerant the growth function is. dt is the time step -- smaller values are more stable but slower.
